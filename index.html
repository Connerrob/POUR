What is a11y?

A11y is a numerical abbreviation that represents the word "accessibility". The term "a11y" comes from the word "accessiblity" by replacing the 11 letters between the "a" and "y" with the number 11.

-- What does accessibility mean in the context of the web?

Accessibility, in the context of the web refers to the inclusive design and developement of websites and digital content to ensure that people with disabilities can perceive, understand, navigate, and interact with them effectively, it involves removing barriers and providing equal access to information, functionality, and services for all users, regardless of their abilities.

What are the POUR Principles?

Perceivable

The perceivable principle focuses on making content accessible to all users, including those with visual or auditory impairments. It emphasizes providing alternatives for non-text content, ensuring content can be presented in different ways (through assistive technology), and making sure content is distinguishable and easy to perceive.

Example Problems

  • Alt text: Tables, images, charts, graphs, and other visual elements not having alt text.
  • Captions and Transcripts: Multimedia content such as videos or music, not having captions or transcripts.
  • Color Contrast: Text that has low color contrast between the text and backgorund elements, making them difficult to read for users with visual impairments.

Example Solutions

  • Alt text: Including a descriptive alternative text on visual elements that have meaningful information. Doing this gives users relying on a screenreader a description of what the image provides.
  • Captions and Transcripts: Captions and transcripts play a crucial role in making audio and video content accessible to individuals who are deaf or hard of hearing. Captions provide a text-based representation of the content, allowing users to read along with the dialogue, sound effects, and other audio elements. Captions and transscripts should accurately and comprehensively represent the spoken content. Ensure that captions include all relevant dialogue, identify speakers when necessary, and convey important non-verbal information, such as sound effects.
  • Color Contrast: Ensure your color contrast is at least 4.5:1 to improving readability for users with visual impairments. You can check the ratio at WebAIM Contrast Checker

Operable

The Operable principle focuses on making content easily operable and navigable. It ensures that users can interact with the content using a variety of input methods and devices. This principle promotes keyboard accessibility, providing sufficient time for users to interact with content, avoiding content that can trigger seizures or physical reactions.

Example Problems

  • Keyboard Accessible: Web content relying on mouse or touch input.
  • Animations: Animations can greatly enhance the user experience on a website, but they can also pose challenges for individuals with certain disabilities.
  • Interactive elements: Interactive elements, such as slideshows, carousels, or timed quizzes, that automatically advance or have time limits.

Example Solutions

  • Keyboard Accessible: Keyboard functionality is an essential part of website accessiblity, as it allows individuals who cannot use a mouse or other pointing device to navigate and interact with web content using only a keyboard. Ensure that interactive elements, such as links, buttons, and form inputs, can receive focus using the “Tab” key. Each focusable element should have a clear and visible focus indicator, such as a highlight or outline, to indicate the currently focused element. Make sure that the keyboard navigation order follows a logical and meaningful sequence.
  • Animations: To ensure accessiblity when using animations, consider allowing users to control and interact with animations. Avoid auto playing animations or transitions that may startle or distract the users. Provide options to pause, stop or adjust the speed of animations. Avoid using animations that include rapid flashing, flickering, or strobing effects, as these can trigger seizures or cause discomfort for individuals with photosensitivity or epilepsy.
  • Interactive elements: Provide controls to pause, stop, or manually navigate through content. Allow users to adjust the timing or disable the automatic advancements

Understandable

The Understandable principle emphasizes making input and content clear and easy to understand. It involves presenting information in a simple and organized manner, using plain language, and providing assistance or instructions when needed. The goal is to ensure that all users can comprehend and navigate the content effectively.

Example Problems

  • Logic: Content lacking hierachy.
  • Clear intructions and error messages: Not providing clear instructions and error messages.
  • Clear and Concise: Websites that use complex or technical language, jargon, or unclear terminology.

Example Solutions

  • Logic: Establish a clear information hierarchy by prioritzing content based on its importance and relevance. Use headings, subheadings, and paragraphs to structure and organizing content.
  • Clear intructions and error messages: Providing clear intructions that explicitly state what the users need to do. Break down instructions into sequential steps if necessary. Consider using visual cues, such as icons or illustrations, to reinforce the intructions and make them more understandable.
  • Clear and Concise language: Using clear and concise language that is easy to understand. Avoid excessive use of technical terms or jargon. Provide definitions or explainations for any specialized terminaology used.

Robust

The Robust principle focuses on ensuring that content is compatible and can be interpreted reliably by a wide range assistive technology.

Example problems

  • Markup: Not using valid and accessible markup.
  • Technology Compatibilty: Lack of compatibilty with assistive technology
  • Browser Compatibilty: Unpredictable or Unsupported behavior across different browsers or devices.

Example Solutions

  • Markup: Using div is easy, but there are tags you can use that may help assistive technology. If possible, use proper elements like header, main, nav, and aside you can have multiple aside tags. Why use these tags instead of using divs? Using these tags help assistive technologies navigate through the content more efficiently. These roles provide structural cues and allow users to jump to specific sections easily.
  • Compatibilty: Test the website's compatibilty with popular assistive technologies, like screen readers, magnifiers, and keyboard navigation.
  • Browser Compatibilty: Conduct thorough testing across multiple browsers, devices, and platforms to ensure consistent and predictable behavior.
